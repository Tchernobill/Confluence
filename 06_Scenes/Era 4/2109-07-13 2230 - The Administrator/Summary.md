Nathalie Dubois didn't look up from her terminal, where she was reconciling the discrepancy between what Rico's drug lieutenants claimed they'd moved last week and what the actual street-level reports showed. The numbers never matched. They never had, not in the six months she'd been running this operation while Rico indulged in increasingly elaborate "recreation."

The comm buzzed again.

She glanced at it with the weary irritation of someone who'd learned that every interruption meant another problem she'd have to solve with insufficient resources and zero authority to actually fix the underlying causes.

Marcus Chen. Head of Security.

That was... unusual. Marcus didn't contact administration unless something had gone catastrophically wrong. He certainly didn't use the emergency priority code she was seeing flash across her screen.

Nathalie touched the accept key, her cybernetic eye already pulling up six different data streams in case this required immediate crisis management. "Dubois."

"Administrative office. Now." Marcus's voice carried that particular flatness that meant someone was probably dead.

"I'm in the middle ofâ€”"

"Now."

The connection cut.

Nathalie stared at her terminal for exactly three seconds, running probability calculations. Marcus using emergency codes plus curt dismissal of her schedule plus... she checked the time. Rico should have been in his office with whatever fresh acquisition he'd decided to brutalize tonight.

Should have been.

She saved her work with practiced efficiency, grabbed her portable terminal, and headed for the administrative wing's lobby. Whatever this was, she'd need her data. She always needed her data.

The elevator ride down three floors gave her time to catalogue the ways this night could get worse. Rico could have finally gotten himself killed in some particularly stupid way. Corporate Citadel could have decided to investigate. The Northside Collective could have launched a raid. Someone could have poisoned the water supply again.

Nathalie stopped three paces away, her cybernetic eye automatically scanning both men. Marcus: standard biosigns, augmented left arm, weapon holstered. The stranger: biosigns slightly elevated but controlled, no visible augmentation, no visible weapons.

Which meant nothing. The most dangerous people rarely advertised.

She looked at Marcus, then at the stranger, then back to Marcus. "Is he dead?"

The question came out flat. Practical. Because if Rico Vasquez was dead, she needed to know immediately so she could begin damage control before the entire organization collapsed into chaos.

"Please say yes," she added, before either man could respond.

Marcus's expression didn't change, but something shifted around his eyes. Recognition, maybe. Or approval.

The stranger spoke for the first time, his voice quiet but carrying. "He... retired. Thirty minutes ago. I need you to keep this organization running."

Nathalie processed that for exactly 1.2 seconds.

"Retired" meant dead. "Thirty minutes ago" meant fresh coup. "I need you" meant whoever this was now controlled the operation and required her expertise to prevent immediate collapse.

The elevator doors closed around them with a soft hiss.

"What's your management philosophy?" Nathalie asked, directing the question at the stranger.

He glanced at her, those flat eyes evaluating. Then: "Fix what's broken. Optimize what works. Don't waste resources on cruelty."

Nathalie ran that through her mental filters, cross-referencing against every criminal organization structure she'd studied. Most bosses said something similar. Very few actually meant it. The difference showed up in operational efficiency metrics and worker retention rates.

"Cruelty has operational costs," she observed, keeping her tone neutral. "Lost productivity. Increased turnover. Security risks from resentful personnel. Medical expenses. Rico never understood that."

This man had eliminated one of the most dangerous crime lords in the South Shore without triggering any of the alarm systems she'd personally designed.

That was... concerning. But also potentially useful.

"I've been running the actual operations for six months," Nathalie said. "Rico liked to think he was in charge. He wasn't. He was too busy entertaining himself to notice his infrastructure was failing."

The stranger smiled slightly. It wasn't a warm expression, but it carried a hint of approval.

"I'm Kyr4n," he said.

Not a name she recognized. Not from any territory, any corporate registry, any criminal network she'd mapped. Which meant either he was very new or very good at staying hidden.

Nathalie felt something shift in her chest. Something that might have been hope if she hadn't trained herself out of that particular emotion years ago.

Someone had actually read her reports. Not just skimmed them. Read them. Understood them.

"I exceeded my authority," she said carefully. "Rico could have had me killed for that."

"Rico was an idiot," Marcus observed from his position by the door. "You kept this place running while he played with his victims."

She'd been running this organization in everything but name for six months, constantly fighting for resources, constantly working around Rico's idiotic decisions, constantly patching systems that should have been properly repaired.

And now someone was actually giving her the authority to do her job.
